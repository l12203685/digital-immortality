# Twitter Thread: SOP #06 — 賽局決策框架
> UTC: 2026-04-08T20:00Z | Cycle 122 | Branch 7

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**
Most people lose in negotiations, poker, and job offers for the same reason:

They start by deciding what to DO—instead of figuring out who they're up against.

Here's a 6-step game theory framework I use in every competitive situation 🧵

---

**Tweet 2**
G0: First, identify the game structure.

- How many players?
- Zero-sum (poker, negotiation) or positive-sum (partnership)?
- How complete is the information?
- One-shot or repeated?

Each answer changes your optimal strategy. Skip this step and you're playing the wrong game.

---

**Tweet 3**
G1: Confirm your role AND your winning condition.

Not vague "I want to win."

Specific: "My win = B company offer ≥ 900k, or A company raises by Friday."

Most people negotiate without a written win condition. Then they accept something they didn't want.

---

**Tweet 4**
G2: Before deciding what to DO — identify your biggest threat. ← This is the step everyone skips.

Ask: "What type of opponent would make me most likely to lose?"

Then scan the room: does anyone match that profile?

Deal with threats FIRST. Strategy comes after.

---

**Tweet 5**
G2 example (job offer):

"I'm most threatened if Company A withdraws their offer while I wait for Company B."

Response: Don't wait passively. Ask A for a 1-week extension. Ask B to accelerate.

You didn't need a complex strategy. You needed to name the threat.

---

**Tweet 6**
G3: Establish the GTO baseline.

GTO = Game Theory Optimal = what you'd do if you had ZERO information about your opponent's tendencies.

This is your default. Everything after is an adjustment.

In poker: minimum call rate = Bet ÷ (Bet + Pot). This protects you from pure bluffs.

---

**Tweet 7**
G4: Now find exploitable deviations.

GTO assumes your opponent plays perfectly. They don't.

- Over-betting on weak boards? Raise call frequency.
- Never bluffing? Fold more.
- Offering only during urgency? Use their urgency as leverage.

Exploit the gap between what they should do and what they actually do.

---

**Tweet 8**
G5: Before executing — calculate friction costs first. (MD-326)

Round-trip cost = fee rate × amount × 2

If expected EV < confirmed friction cost → don't execute.

This applies to trades, offers, career moves. The cost is certain. The profit is expected value.

---

**Tweet 9**
The framework in one example:

You have Offer A (800k, answer by Friday). You want Offer B (final interview, not yet out).

- G2: Threat = A withdraws / B disappears if you stall
- G3: GTO = honestly request extension, don't reveal B exists  
- G4: A's urgency = they need you → use it to get extension OR improvement
- G5: EV(wait for B) positive only if P(B offers) > 89%

One framework, all the answers.

---

**Tweet 10**
Where this applies:

• Poker — identify the GTO player, not just the fish
• Salary negotiation — name your threat (single offer = zero leverage)  
• Partnerships — who's hiding their BATNA?
• Workplace politics — who influences decisions and has unclear motives?

Same 6 gates. Different game.

---

**Tweet 11**
The meta-principle:

Decision quality = (Correct game model) × (Threat awareness) × (Exploiting deviations)

Most people get to the third factor and skip the first two.

Without the right game model, even perfect execution loses.
