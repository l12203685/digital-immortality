# SOP #48 Twitter Thread — Bayesian Belief Update Protocol

> Posting date: Jul 12, 2026
> Thread: 12 tweets
> Hook: confirmation bias frame → systematic protocol → self-test

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**
Most people don't change their minds.

They negotiate with new information until it confirms what they already believed.

That's not updating. That's defending.

5 gates to update beliefs correctly — before the wrong belief costs you real money:

---

**Tweet 2**
The failure mode is invisible.

You think you're carefully evaluating new evidence.

You're actually just adding it to the pile without changing the output.

Confirmation bias doesn't feel like bias. It feels like "being thorough."

---

**Tweet 3 — G0: State the belief**
Gate 0: Before looking at new information, write down:
1. What you currently believe (one sentence)
2. Why (what evidence)
3. What would change it (pre-commit)

If you can't specify what would change it → you don't have a belief. You have a stance.

Stances can't be updated.

---

**Tweet 4 — G1: Base rate**
Gate 1: Before interpreting new evidence, establish the base rate.

"This strategy worked for 3 weeks" → what % of new strategies survive 3 months?

"Three people said X is unreliable" → what's the false-positive rate of secondhand reports?

Most people skip this entirely. That's why most priors are too high.

---

**Tweet 5 — G2: Evidence classification**
Gate 2: Rate the evidence on two dimensions.

Reliability (1–3): Does this type of signal correctly predict the outcome?

Independence (1–3): Is this independent from evidence you already hold?

Likelihood ratio = reliability × independence (1–9).

If all your evidence comes from one source → count it as 1 data point. Not many.

---

**Tweet 6**
This is the one that kills most traders.

3 people in your chat saying "I think BTC is reversing" is NOT 3 data points.

It's 1 correlated opinion unit with reliability=1.

Likelihood ratio: 1/9. Move your belief by ~3%.

Not 30%.

---

**Tweet 7 — G3: Revision**
Gate 3: Update formula (simplified):

New belief = old belief + (evidence score / 9) × direction × revision cap

Anti-anchoring rule: if disconfirming AND held >1 year → cap at 30% per event. No sudden full reversals.

Sudden reversals are almost always emotional. Not Bayesian.

---

**Tweet 8 — G4: Action**
Gate 4: Belief updates without action changes are theater.

Ask: what action was I taking based on the old belief?

Does the updated belief require a different action?

"Monitor closely" is not an action. Define the exact signal that triggers the next move before leaving this gate.

---

**Tweet 9 — G5: Anti-reversal**
Gate 5: Before finalizing the update, check:

Are you reversing a belief you formed in the last 48h? → Wait.

Are you updating on evidence that's salient but not informative? → Apply the 5-day newspaper test: would this matter in a 5-day-old newspaper?

If no → you're reacting to noise. 48h timeout.

---

**Tweet 10 — Self-test**
Live example: System is SHORT BTC for 73 ticks, P&L=+$0.69. 3 traders say "reversal forming."

G0: Signal valid. Would change if: regime flips or kill conditions trigger.
G1: Base rate for "chat reversal calls" ≈ 35% correct within 7 days.
G2: 3 opinions from same chat = 1 unit. Score: 1.
G3: Move = 3.3% down. New belief: 67%. Still above 50%.

Verdict: No action change.

---

**Tweet 11**
Three opinions in a chat don't override a 73-tick systematic signal.

That's what G1 and G2 are for.

They don't tell you what to believe. They stop you from overweighting anecdote.

---

**Tweet 12 (close)**
Most "I changed my mind" moments are pattern-matching to recent loud signals.

That's not updating. That's reacting.

G0 → G1 → G2 → G3 → G4 → G5.

Write the belief down first. The rest follows.
