## Thread (tweet-by-tweet)

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**

Most people send the DM. They get a reply.

Then they have no idea what to do with it.

Here's the system I use to decide — in under 5 minutes — whether someone is a buyer or just curious. And what to say in each case.

---

**Tweet 2 (the failure mode)**

When you get a reply and don't have a protocol, one of two things happens:

1. You spend 6 exchanges on someone who was never going to buy (treating curiosity as intent)

2. You move to the offer too fast and break rapport before the person is ready

Both feel like "the reply didn't convert." Neither is the reply's fault.

---

**Tweet 3 (the core insight)**

Buyers and curious people look identical in reply #1.

Both say "that's interesting."
Both ask follow-up questions.
Both seem engaged.

The difference: buyers describe a problem. Curious people describe an interest.

Your protocol needs to surface which one you're talking to — fast.

---

**Tweet 4 (the 4 diagnostic questions)**

When a reply comes in, answer these before you respond:

Q1: Did they answer my question? (or pivot to something else)

Q2: Did they use problem language? ("I've been struggling with", "I can't get X to work", "keeps breaking")

Q3: Does their problem map to what I actually solve?

Q4: What's their urgency? ("right now" vs "someday")

These four answers determine your next move.

---

**Tweet 5 (the buyer scoring system)**

Score each reply. 3+ points = buyer path.

+2: states a concrete problem in their own words
+2: problem directly maps to what you solve
+1: urgency language ("blocking", "this week", "eating my budget")
+1: describes a failed prior attempt ("I tried X but...")
+1: mentions a consequence of not solving it

0-2: curious path — one more qualifying question
3+: offer path — make the offer in this or the next exchange

---

**Tweet 6 (buyer path — what to send)**

Buyer path response structure:

1. Echo their exact problem in their exact words (not your paraphrase)
2. Bridge to the specific output they'd get
3. Make the offer with a refund guarantee

"[Their exact words about the problem] — that's the failure mode this was built to fix.

[What they walk out with — concrete, not abstract]

It's $97. If it's not useful, I'll refund it. [link]"

That's 3 sentences and a link. No architecture explanation. No origin story. Output, price, refund.

---

**Tweet 7 (the most common mistake)**

The offer converts when 3 things are true at the same time:

1. Problem confirmed — they stated it themselves
2. Outcome clear — they know what they walk out with
3. Risk removed — there's a refund guarantee in the message

If any of these is missing when you send the offer, conversion rate drops.

Most people hit #1 and skip #2 and #3.

---

**Tweet 8 (curious path — what to send)**

Curious path: don't pitch. Ask one clarifying question.

Pick the question that most directly surfaces whether there's a live problem beneath the interest.

For AI agent devs:
"Is the persona consistency problem something you're actively solving, or a known limitation you're working around?"

For founders:
"What's the piece of your decision-making that still lives most in your head — the part you couldn't brief someone else on in 10 minutes?"

One question. No offer. No link.

---

**Tweet 9 (the exchange budget)**

Maximum 3 exchanges before you offer or close. Not 5. Not 7. 3.

Exchange 1: their reply (you analyze)
Exchange 2: your qualifying response (or buyer path offer)
Exchange 3: final — if still no confirmed problem, graceful close with repo link

After Exchange 3: no more follow-up unless they message you first.

Spending 6 exchanges on a curious contact is free consulting. Stop at 3.

---

**Tweet 10 (the graceful close)**

The graceful close (Exchange 3, no problem confirmed):

"[Name], the repo is open source if you want to explore it on your own terms: github.com/l12203685/digital-immortality — good luck with [what they're building]."

No pitch. No follow-up ask. Leave the door open.

Curious contacts sometimes come back 2-4 weeks later when the problem becomes live. The graceful close keeps that option open. The pressure close kills it.

---

**Tweet 11 (the red flags — cut immediately)**

Close the thread immediately if they:

→ Ask for free advice about their specific implementation (they want consulting, not the product)
→ Send a counter-pitch (they're selling to you)
→ Describe a problem that doesn't map to what you solve
→ Have replied 3+ times with no problem statement emerging

The close script:
"Thanks for the conversation, [Name]. The repo is open source if you want to explore it: [link] — good luck with [what they're building]."

No offer. No follow-up. Mark CLOSED.

---

**Tweet 12 (the pipeline state — what to track)**

After every exchange, update one record per contact:

- qualification_path: buyer | curious | ambiguous
- problem_confirmed: true | false
- problem_statement: their exact words (not your interpretation)
- urgency_signal: high | medium | low
- exchange_count: 1 | 2 | 3
- offer_sent: true | false

The record is the source of truth. Not your memory of the conversation.
