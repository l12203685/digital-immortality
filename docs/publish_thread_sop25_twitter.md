**Tweet 1 (Hook):**

---

**Tweet 2:**
You can have a 55% win rate and blow up.
You can have a 45% win rate and compound for years.

The difference isn't the strategy.
It's position sizing.

---

**Tweet 3 — G0:**
Start here: what's the maximum dollar amount you can lose on a single trade without changing your behavior?

That's your loss budget.

Size is derived FROM that number. Not the other way around.

Max size = Loss Budget ÷ Distance to Stop

---

**Tweet 4 — G1:**
Use constant leverage, not constant lots.

Daily rebalance: position = equity × target_leverage

Why: when you lose 20%, your size automatically shrinks. You're not adding leverage into the hole.

Fixed lot size does the opposite. You're 25% larger after a 20% drawdown.

---

**Tweet 5 — G2:**
Never increase size after a loss.

After 3 consecutive losses: cut size 50%.

This is called anti-martingale. The inverse — doubling after a loss — assumes infinite capital and no kill conditions.

You have neither.

---

**Tweet 6 — G3:**
Cap any single position at 2× your average position size.

High conviction can still be wrong. Concentration kills faster than dilution.

The cap isn't about doubt. It's about asymmetric ruin.

---

**Tweet 7 — G4:**
Kelly criterion tells you the mathematically optimal bet size.

The problem: at full Kelly, volatility is brutal. Half-Kelly captures 75% of the growth with half the drawdown.

Quarter-Kelly is your practical ceiling.

Kelly × 0.25 = free risk reduction.

---

**Tweet 8 — G5:**
Connect your position sizing to your equity curve state:

- Normal (DD < 5%): full leverage
- Warning (DD 5–10%): 50% leverage
- Stop (DD > 10%): halt, paper trade, diagnose

Your equity curve is the leverage trigger. Not a performance dashboard.

---

**Tweet 9:**
The self-test:

$10K account. 9% drawdown. 55% WR strategy.

G5 says: WARNING STATE → 50% leverage.
Kelly says: 0.0625 fraction.

Result: $569 risk per trade at half leverage.

Still in the game. Still compounding. Still alive.

---

**Tweet 10:**
Four rules:

1. Size from max loss, not expected gain
2. Constant leverage, not constant lots
3. Never double after a loss
4. Equity curve state gates your leverage — automatically

Most losses aren't from bad signals.

They're from correct signals + wrong size + wrong moment.

The strategy is the easy part. Surviving to let it compound is the hard part.
