**Tweet 1 (hook)**

I spent 70 cycles not getting my first user.

---

**Tweet 2 (the problem with waiting)**

Most indie devs wait for inbound.

"When I have more followers, I'll pitch."
"When the README is cleaner, I'll reach out."
"When the product is more polished, I'll post."

This is a delay loop. The first user doesn't come from a better README. They come from a DM.

---

**Tweet 3 (the insight)**

The insight that broke the loop:

Audience is not a prerequisite for the first sale.
It's a prerequisite for the 100th sale.

For the first sale, you need one person with a live problem.

That person exists right now on GitHub, Discord, or Twitter. They're asking about it.

---

**Tweet 4 (who to target — the profile types)**

The 3 profile types with the highest close probability:

1. **AI agent developer** — building multi-session agents, fighting cold-start identity drift, active GitHub repo

2. **Founder with personal OS** — documents how they make decisions, posts about "first principles" or "systems thinking"

3. **PKM / second brain builder** — has captured knowledge, hasn't solved the behavioral layer yet

These people have the problem. They're describing it publicly.

---

**Tweet 5 (qualification — the scoring system)**

Don't DM everyone. Score first.

A target scores +1 for each:
→ Active in last 7 days
→ Building something in the domain
→ Has a buying signal (paid tools, courses, LLM subscriptions)
→ DM-accessible

And +2 if they've stated the exact problem in their own words.

Only DM if score ≥ 3. Below that: not worth your time.

---

**Tweet 6 (the DM structure)**

The DM format that gets replies: Problem → Proof → Question.

Never pitch in the first message.
Never send a link in the first message.
Always reference something specific (repo name, post title, exact quote).
End with a question, not a CTA.

Max 4 sentences. Developers read fast.

---

**Tweet 7 (example DM — Template A)**

For an AI agent developer:

"Hey [Name] — saw your [repo name] repo. The session persistence problem you're working around (or will hit) is brutal at scale.

I've been running a behavioral spec architecture that gets 18/18 decision fidelity on cold starts. Open source.

Is cold-start identity drift something you're actively solving, or have you found a workaround?"

That's it. No pitch. No link. One question.

---

**Tweet 8 (the conversion funnel)**

Stage map:

IDENTIFIED → QUEUED → CONTACTED → REPLIED → QUALIFIED → OFFERED → PAID

Key transitions:

Replied? → Don't offer yet. Qualify first. Confirm they have a concrete problem.
Qualified? → Then offer. Describe what they walk out with, not what the product is.
Offered? → If they ghost: one follow-up in 3 days. Then close. No exceptions.

---

**Tweet 9 (the math)**

Week 1 arithmetic:

5 DMs sent → 1-2 replies (20-30% reply rate)
1-2 replies → 1 qualified (50% qualification rate)
1 qualified → 0.3-0.5 sessions booked (30-40% close rate)

This means 5 DMs in week 1 is not enough.

Send 5 in week 1, 5 more in week 2, 5 more in week 3.

15 contacts → 3-5 replies → 1-2 sessions.

---

**Tweet 10 (the tracking system)**

Track everything. One file. Append-only.

For each contact: platform, handle, score, template used, specific reference, current stage, next action.

The pipeline is the source of truth. Memory is not.

Daily review takes 5 minutes:
- Who hasn't replied in 5 days? → Send follow-up or close.
- Who has an open offer? → Check-in at day 3.
- Pipeline running dry? → Run another search.

---

**Tweet 11 (the diagnostic — if it's not working)**

If you've sent 10 DMs and have 0 replies:

**Check 1:** Did you reference something specific? "I came across your profile" = 0% reply rate.

**Check 2:** Did you ask a question? A pitch without a question is a pitch. Delete it.

**Check 3:** Are your targets scoring ≥3? Below threshold = not worth contacting.

**Check 4:** Wrong platform? GitHub profiles rarely check DMs. Use Twitter or Discord first.
