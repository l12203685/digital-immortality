# Twitter Thread — SOP #12: Social Capital & Network Design

**Hook:**
Most people manage relationships reactively.
They respond when contacted. They reconnect when they need something.
They let silence accumulate until the relationship quietly expires.

That's not friendship. That's slow divestment.
Here's the maintenance protocol I actually run:

---

**1/**
Every relationship has a decay rate.

Left unattended, even the best relationships depreciate.
The silence period is the key variable — not how you feel about the person, but how long since you last reached out.

First step: write it down.
10–15 key nodes. Name, tier, last contact date.

If it's not in writing, it's not being managed.

---

**2/**
Tiers have thresholds. Here are mine:

Intimate → 2 weeks max silence
Important friend / peer → 1 month max silence
Career mentor / advisor → 3 months max silence

Every week, I scan the list.
Any node past threshold → touchpoint before the next scan.

One weekly scan. That's the entire maintenance habit.

---

**3/**
The most common objection:
"But I don't have anything to say."

You don't need anything to say.

- "Saw this and thought of you." (45 seconds)
- "How's [thing they mentioned last time] going?" (20 seconds)
- "It's been too long. Hope you're well." (10 seconds)

Proactive maintenance is not transactional.
Its job is presence, not value extraction.

---

**4/**
The worst relationship pattern:

Silence → need arises → contact → ask → silence again.

That's not maintaining a relationship.
That's using one.

The person on the receiving end feels the difference.
And the relationship decays *faster* after transactional contact than after no contact at all.

---

**5/**
When entering any new domain relevant to a relationship — learn the map before the territory.

Framework-first, always.

20% of time → build the skeleton (TOC, overview, 3–5 core concepts)
80% of time → fill in details with anchor points already set

Reading without a map = storing information with no retrieval trigger.
You'll have read the book and retained the feeling of having learned.

---

**6/**
The insight that changed how I evaluate new relationships:

Language is cheap. Behavior is expensive.

When someone tells you what they'll do, that's data about their intentions.
When they do it — or don't — that's data about their character.

When the two diverge, behavior carries almost all the information.

---

**7/**
The 3-month observation window.

For any new relationship with material consequence (collaborator, mentor, potential partner):
- Log verbal commitments as they happen
- Track whether they were followed through

At 3 months, calculate:
Fulfillment rate = commitments kept / commitments made

---

**8/**
The thresholds:

≥ 80% → speech and behavior aligned. Weight their words at face value.
50–79% → willing but inconsistent. Keep observing; don't depend on follow-through.
< 50% → structural gap. Discount speech weight to near-zero.

This isn't a moral judgment.
It's a calibration.
It tells you what kind of reliance is appropriate.

---

**9/**
The scenario that makes this concrete:

New potential trading partner. Enthusiastic at the conference.
Proposes collaboration. Promises introductions. Wants to "set up a call."

Old approach: assume the enthusiasm is real → plan around it.

SOP approach:
- Log every commitment made that day
- Observe for 3 months
- Calculate fulfillment rate
- Make the decision with behavioral data, not first-impression data

---

**10/**
Once a year: audit your network against your current goals.

Not "do I like these people" — that's the wrong question.
The right questions:
- For each major goal this year, do I have 2–3 nodes with direct relevant experience?
- Am I over-concentrated in one domain?
- Who, if added, would most accelerate where I'm trying to go?
- What am I maintaining out of inertia rather than genuine value?

Network composition is a career infrastructure decision.

---

**11/**
The failure table:

Important relationship gone 6 months silent → no weekly scan installed
Relied on someone who didn't follow through → skipped the observation window
Learned a domain but can't retrieve anything → consumed detail without a skeleton first
Network is large but nobody can help → maintaining inertia, not current goals
Reach out only when you need something → contact is transactional

One gate missed = the whole system leaks.

---

**12/**
The core reframe:

Relationships are assets.
Assets have decay rates.
Assets require active management.
Active management runs on a schedule, not a feeling.

"Networking more" is a volume strategy applied to a composition problem.
The real question is not how many — it's whether your network matches where you're going.

SOP #12 of my Digital Immortality series — how I build systems that survive past me.
