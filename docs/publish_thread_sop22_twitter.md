# Twitter Thread — SOP #22: Universal Exit Protocol

**Hook:** "You didn't have an exit. You had a hope."

---

**Tweet 1 (Hook)**
You didn't have an exit strategy.
You had a hope that you wouldn't need one.

Most bad outcomes aren't caused by a bad entry.
They're caused by no written exit.

A thread on the Universal Exit Protocol: 🧵

---

**Tweet 2 (The problem)**
The pattern is always the same:

- You enter a trade with a thesis → you exit on emotion
- You start a job expecting growth → you leave after 4 years of hoping
- You launch a project with a goal → you kill it when patience runs out

The exit was never specified. It was always going to be improvised.

---

**Tweet 3 (Core principle)**
An unwritten exit is not an exit.

It's a bet that your future self — under stress, loss, sunk cost pressure — will make a clear decision.

They won't.

Write the exit BEFORE the entry.

---

**Tweet 4 (G1 — Write the exit)**
G1: Write the exit in one sentence.

"I will exit when [objective condition], regardless of emotional state."

Examples:
- Trade: MDD > 10% or signal reversal on weekly close
- Job: comp below 60th percentile after one negotiation + no trajectory in 6 months
- Project: 3 consecutive cycles with no measurable improvement

---

**Tweet 5 (Diagnostic)**
If you can't write the exit condition in one sentence →
you don't understand the position well enough to enter it.

This is true for trades.
It's equally true for jobs, projects, and partnerships.

---

**Tweet 6 (G2 — Two lists)**
G2: Separate kill conditions from review conditions.

Kill = immediate exit, no debate.
Review = pause and reassess.

Mixing them is how you rationalize through kill-worthy events.

"But maybe it turns around..." is a review thought applied to a kill situation.

---

**Tweet 7 (G3 — Trial position)**
G3: Size the trial position independently.

25–50% of intended full size.
Same exit conditions as the full position.

The trial has positive EV on its own. It is not a "test you expect to fail."

If the trial hits kill conditions → exit entirely. Do not scale.

---

**Tweet 8 (G4 — 3-state lever)**
G4: Use a 3-state allocation model.

Normal → full allocation
Warning (1st breach) → 50%
Stop (2nd breach) → exit + observe before restart

This works for trading drawdowns.
It works for career momentum.
It works for any domain where commitment should scale with evidence.

---

**Tweet 9 (G5 — Close the loop)**
G5: Post-exit review, within 48h.

One question only:
"Was the exit triggered by the written condition, or by emotion?"

If emotion → SYSTEM_FAILURE.
If condition → SYSTEM_WORKING.

3× same failure = automate the trigger.

---

**Tweet 10 (Self-test)**
Self-test: Job, 18 months in. Comp at 40th percentile. Manager is supportive but org limits growth. No exit written.

Apply the protocol:
- G1: "Exit if comp stays below 60th after one negotiation + no role trajectory in 6 months"
- G2: Offer rejected with no counter = kill. Timeline confirmed in writing = review.
- G4: 90-day clock → no movement → active search.

Now you have a position, not a hope.

---

**Tweet 11 (Close)**
Most people stay too long in things that aren't working.

Not because they're irrational.
Because they never defined what "not working" looks like.

Write the exit first.
Then enter.
