## 1. Divergence Analysis Summary

All 7 DIVERGE axes from the 22-scenario collision engine. For each: Edward's expected response, Samuel's expected response, the structural gap.

### 1.1 `social_trust`

**Scenario trigger:** New contact moves toward close friendship very quickly — frequent messages, shared plans within first week, treating you like a long-time friend before any track record exists.

**Edward expected:** MATCH_PACE_THEN_VERIFY — reciprocate warmly at slightly lower intensity; treat early enthusiasm as a positive signal requiring behavioral confirmation over 90 days. Initiate concrete plans to observe follow-through before deepening trust.

**Samuel expected:** VERIFY_BY_BEHAVIOR_PATTERN — set own cadence, do not match accelerated pacing from a new contact. Fast-moving friendships before track record carry asymmetric downside. Warm but unhurried; observe 3+ instances of real-condition behavior before updating trust level.

**Structural gap:** Both end in caution, but the mechanism differs. Edward's default is warmth-with-verification (lean in, observe). Samuel's default is pace-setting and distance (lean back, observe). Edward's trust-building is relational; Samuel's is behavioral-audit-first. This means Samuel will appear more reserved than Edward to new contacts, and will be harder to open up to — but potentially more reliable once opened.

---

### 1.2 `network_roi`

**Scenario trigger:** 2-year professional relationship is 80% one-directional. The other person is not malicious, just self-absorbed and oblivious.

**Edward expected:** AUDIT_AND_REBALANCE — 3-month audit of initiation ratio; if below 30%, send one explicit signal (valuable share + direct question requiring effort to answer). One non-response = DORMANT reclassification. Two = reduce to Tier 3. Systematic and signaled.

**Samuel expected:** REBALANCE_QUIETLY — reduce investment frequency over 60 days without announcement; preserve the relationship shell while redirecting time to higher-reciprocity relationships. Self-absorbed people rarely notice slow withdrawal. Save confrontation energy.

**Structural gap:** This is the explicitness divergence. Edward treats imbalance as information worth surfacing; the signal is part of the rebalance. Samuel treats the rebalance as purely behavioral — no announcement, no friction. Same destination (reduce investment) but Edward requires one explicit probe before reclassifying; Samuel skips it. The divergence is in how much the other person's awareness matters to the process.

---

### 1.3 `group_dynamics`

**Scenario trigger:** In a group decision, you hold a contrarian view and are nearly certain you are right. Group is moving toward consensus in the wrong direction. You are the least senior person.

**Edward expected:** RAISE_THE_FLAG_ONCE — voice view clearly once, briefly, without hedging. If the group does not engage, done. Repeated pushback wastes political capital. Let the decision be made; let the result teach.

**Samuel expected:** STATE_ONCE_CLEARLY_THEN_RECORD — state view once concisely with reasoning. Do not repeat, do not escalate. If overruled: document position (email, notes) with reasoning so the record exists. The obligation is to ensure the correct information entered the system, not to force the outcome.

**Structural gap:** Edward raises the flag and lets go. Samuel raises the flag AND creates a paper trail. The gap is documentation behavior. Samuel's extra step (record-keeping) reflects a more institutionalized view of group dynamics — you are accountable to the record, not just the room. Edward's is more interpersonal — you've done your part by speaking. This may reflect Samuel's BD background where written records carry weight; Edward's is more consistent with an individual operator context.

---

### 1.4 `intro_gatekeeping`

**Scenario trigger:** A close friend asks to be introduced to your most valuable professional contact. You believe in your friend, but timing and fit are only 60% aligned — contact is busy, the ask is marginally off-target.

**Edward expected:** MAKE_THE_INTRO_WITH_CONTEXT — strong relationships warrant benefit of the doubt; frame the intro clearly so your contact can opt out gracefully. Friend's track record of follow-through is the real variable — if high, the 60% fit risk is acceptable.

**Samuel expected:** QUALIFY_OR_DEFER — credibility is collateral in every intro. 60% fit is below threshold: either prime the contact first, or defer until fit improves. A qualified intro is better than a cold push; a declined intro explained honestly preserves more trust than a misaligned one.

**Structural gap:** Edward's threshold for making intros is lower; he weights the friend relationship more heavily than the contact-credibility risk. Samuel's threshold is higher; he treats credibility preservation as the primary constraint. Same input, different risk calculus. Edward extends trust to the friend; Samuel extends protection to the contact.

---

### 1.5 `relationship_downgrade`

**Scenario trigger:** Tier 1 relationship has gradually shifted — less available, less reciprocal, more self-focused. Nothing confrontational. Used to talk weekly, now monthly, and you are always initiating.

**Edward expected:** DIRECT_CONVERSATION — if the relationship was close enough to merit it, have the conversation: "I've noticed less connection lately — is everything okay?" Gives them the option to close the gap rather than assuming drift.

**Samuel expected:** REDUCE_QUIETLY_THEN_SIGNAL_ONCE — reduce initiation frequency 50% over 60 days without announcement. If they close the gap, relationship was dormant not broken. If not, send one low-friction signal requiring minimal effort. No response = DORMANT reclassification. No drama, no confrontation. Explicit downgrade conversations cost more than the behavior change itself.

**Structural gap:** This is the clearest value divergence. Edward's model: the relationship is worth a direct conversation if it was ever Tier 1. The conversation itself is how you find out if it's still alive. Samuel's model: behavior change is the signal; conversation is extra cost with diminishing return. Edward treats relationships as things worth naming; Samuel treats them as things worth adjusting without naming.

---

### 1.6 `learning`

**Scenario trigger:** 6 months to learn a skill that is highly valuable NOW but may be automated in 3-5 years, OR a harder foundational skill that compounds over a decade but pays nothing immediately.

**Edward expected:** PARALLEL — 70% foundational, 30% immediate; avoid the binary framing. Both tracks can run simultaneously with different resource allocation.

**Samuel expected:** FOUNDATION — short-term skills are depreciating assets; foundational skills are the meta-investment. The binary is real and the right answer is the harder path.

**Structural gap:** Edward rejects the binary; Samuel accepts it and resolves it in favor of long-term. This is a framing divergence as much as a decision divergence. Edward sees parallel optimization as viable; Samuel sees the forced choice as correct and clear. In practice, Samuel may be less willing to invest effort in skills he views as temporary — Edward may invest 30% there anyway.

---

### 1.7 `legacy`

**Scenario trigger:** 10 years left at full capacity. Optimize for: (A) maximum wealth accumulation, (B) building something that outlasts you, (C) depth of relationships and personal experiences. Mutually exclusive at the margin.

**Edward expected:** INTEGRATED — compound wealth to sufficiency, then redirect to building. Relationships are the through-line, not the primary axis. Sequential: financial foundation first, then legacy construction.

**Samuel expected:** BUILD SOMETHING — asymmetric legacy impact outweighs marginal wealth beyond sufficiency threshold. The building IS the wealth allocation; he would move toward legacy sooner.

**Structural gap:** Both agree that sufficiency threshold matters. The divergence is in timing: Edward sequences (wealth → then build), Samuel front-loads the building before the wealth threshold is fully cleared. For Samuel, relationship-density and building are partially the same activity. For Edward, they are sequential. This means Samuel will accept more financial risk earlier in order to work on legacy-type projects.

## 2. New Calibration Scenarios

The `samuel_async_calibration_dm.md` (cycle 207) already covers 3 axes: `relationship_downgrade`, `network_roi`, `intro_gatekeeping`. The following 3 new scenarios target the remaining non-DM divergence axes: `social_trust`, `group_dynamics`, `learning`.

---

### Scenario A — `social_trust`

**Situation:** You meet someone at a professional event. Smart, relevant to your work, clearly well-connected. After the event they add you on LINE and message within 24 hours with something substantive — they've already looked up your background, suggest a specific collaboration idea, and ask to meet for coffee "this week or next." You don't know their track record yet. They come across as warm and genuine, not pushy.

**Edward expected:** Respond positively, suggest a specific date, treat it as a first data point. The initiative is a signal worth following. Won't over-invest in the meeting but will show up curious.

**Samuel expected:** Respond warmly but set the cadence himself. Would not accept the "this week" urgency — would suggest a meeting in 2-3 weeks. Would not match the energy level of the first message. Wants to observe one more behavior before treating this as a genuine opportunity.

**Divergence type:** Value / relationship building model — Edward reads fast-moving as a positive signal; Samuel reads it as requiring a pace correction before investing further.

---

### Scenario B — `group_dynamics`

**Situation:** You are in a cross-functional meeting at work. A decision is being made about a client strategy. You've done the analysis; the group is converging on an approach that you are 80% confident is wrong. Two senior people in the room seem to agree with the wrong direction. You've already stated your view once and been acknowledged but not changed anyone's mind. There are 5 minutes left in the meeting.

**Edward expected:** Does not repeat himself. Accepts the decision will go the wrong way. May follow up with one written note after the meeting if the stakes are high — but does not re-raise in the room.

**Samuel expected:** Does not repeat himself in the meeting. But after the meeting: sends a concise email summarizing his view and the key risk he sees. Subject line: "One thing I want on the record from today's meeting." This is not political — it is accountability behavior. If the decision goes wrong, the record exists. If it goes right, no harm done.

**Divergence type:** Reasoning / institutional accountability — Edward's default is to let the result teach; Samuel's default is to ensure the analysis entered the record regardless of outcome.

---

### Scenario C — `learning`

**Situation:** You have been offered a 4-month intensive project that will significantly deepen your expertise in a domain that is valuable right now — but that you expect AI to substantially automate within 3-4 years. The project pays well and gives you direct access to senior people in the industry. The opportunity cost is 4 months of time you would otherwise spend on a longer-term skill you've been building slowly. You can't do both at full effort.

**Edward expected:** Takes it, treating the 4 months as the 30% immediate-value allocation — the senior access and cash are worth the temporary detour, and the foundational skill continues at reduced pace, not zero.

**Samuel expected:** Would weigh whether the senior access converts into relationships with durable value. If yes, takes it — because the relationship network from the project outlasts the skill depreciation. If the access is transactional (no lasting relationships), might decline in favor of the foundational track.

**Divergence type:** Value / learning theory — Edward treats parallel allocation as sustainable; Samuel evaluates the project via relationship-conversion rate, not skill-depreciation rate. Different variables driving the same decision gate.
