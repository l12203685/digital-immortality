# SOP #37 — Relationship Investment Protocol: Proactive Maintenance + Signal Verification

**Domain:** 4 — 社交圈 (Social Capital)
**Date:** 2026-04-09 UTC
**DNA Anchors:** MD-328, MD-330

---

## Introduction

Most people manage their relationships the way an amateur manages cash: they only check the balance when they need to spend. Relationships are not a checking account — they are assets that decay under neglect and compound under consistent maintenance. This SOP enforces two structural conditions that separate a durable relationship portfolio from one that slowly hollows out. The first: proactive maintenance is not optional and cannot be outsourced to the other party. The second: verbal commitments are hypotheses; only behavior confirms them. Running both gates systematically is what separates social capital that holds under stress from social capital that evaporates exactly when you need it.

---

## Gate G0 — Relationship Asset Mapping

**Trigger:** Once at initialization; revisit annually or after any major life transition.

**Action:** List 10–15 active relationship nodes. For each node, assign a tier.

**Tier taxonomy:**

| Tier | Label | Definition | Examples |
|------|-------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | Intimate | People you would call at 2 AM in a real crisis | partner, closest 1–2 friends, key family |
| 2 | Important | People whose loss would meaningfully degrade your life or work | close friends, career mentors, key collaborators |
| 3 | Peripheral | People worth maintaining but not load-bearing | extended professional network, occasional collaborators |

**Rule (MD-328):** Relationship density = contact frequency × contact quality. Tier determines the maintenance cadence, not the depth of any single interaction. A Tier 1 relationship maintained at Tier 3 frequency decays toward Tier 3 regardless of its history.

**Action:**
1. Write the node list — do not recall from memory. If producing the list takes more than 10 minutes, the portfolio has not been actively managed.
2. Assign each node a tier using the taxonomy above. Tier reflects actual function over the past 12 months, not historical significance or aspirational future role.
3. Record the last meaningful contact date for each node. "Meaningful" = a direct, substantive interaction. A like or passive observation does not count.

**Pass criteria:** A written list of 10–15 nodes exists. Each node has a tier and a last-contact date. The list can be produced in under 10 minutes.

**Kill condition:** If fewer than 3 nodes survive into Tier 1 → you are in social capital deficit. Do not advance to G2. Audit whether Tier 1 relationships have been neglected past their silence ceiling (see G1) and whether the decay is recoverable. Running G2 on an empty Tier 1 is maintenance theater.

## Gate G1 — Silence Period Audit

**Trigger:** Weekly — every 7 days. Also triggered immediately after G0 is completed for the first time.

**Action:** For each tier, enforce a maximum silence period — the longest stretch of zero meaningful contact before the relationship is considered at-risk.

**Silence ceiling by tier:**

| Tier | Max Silence Period | Rationale |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| 1 — Intimate | 2 weeks | These relationships are load-bearing. They cannot be left on autopilot. |
| 2 — Important | 1 month | Regular signal is required to maintain trust and mutual awareness. |
| 3 — Peripheral | 3 months | Tolerates longer gaps; indefinite silence still degrades the node. |

**Rule (MD-328):** "He should message me first" is not a maintenance strategy — it is outsourcing the relationship to the other party. The person with higher relationship capital awareness bears the maintenance responsibility. If that is you, the cadence is yours to own.

**Audit action:**
1. Check each node's last meaningful contact date against its tier's silence ceiling.
2. Flag every node that has exceeded its ceiling. Flagged nodes advance immediately to G2 — they are not deferred to next week.
3. If more than half of Tier 2 nodes are simultaneously overdue, the weekly scan habit has broken down. Reset the cadence before proceeding.

**Pass criteria:** No Tier 1 or Tier 2 node is over its silence ceiling. Tier 3 nodes are triaged: maintain the top 5–7 most valuable Tier 3 nodes; deprioritize the rest if attention is constrained.

**Kill condition:** If 2 or more Tier 1 nodes are simultaneously past ceiling → this is a structural neglect pattern, not an isolated miss. Stop and re-run G0. The tier assignments may be wrong, or the maintenance habit has collapsed. Do not proceed to G2 until the root cause is identified.

## Gate G2 — Proactive Maintenance Trigger

**Trigger:** Weekly, every 7 days. Any node flagged at G1 as overdue triggers G2 before the scan session ends.

**Action:** For every overdue node flagged at G1: initiate contact before closing the session. Do not schedule it forward.

**Rule (MD-328):** Contact content need not be purposeful. The value of proactive maintenance is frequency, not instrumentality. "I saw this and thought of you" carries more relationship capital than a quarterly contact driven by need. The contact itself is the signal — it demonstrates that the relationship exists outside of transactions.

**Contact format by tier:**

| Tier | Minimum contact form | What does not count |
|------|---------------------|---------------------|
| 1 — Intimate | Real conversation: voice, in-person, or substantive written exchange | A text acknowledgment, a meme forward, or a like |
| 2 — Important | Direct message substantive enough to require or invite a response | A forward or passive social signal |
| 3 — Peripheral | Any direct signal: article share, brief note, comment on their work | Likes, views, passive observation |

**Anti-pattern (MD-328):** "Reaching out looks desperate" = conflating proactive maintenance with transactional solicitation. These are categorically different. Proactive maintenance is relationship hygiene. Solicitation is signaling need. Anyone who interprets unprompted maintenance contact as desperation is providing behavioral data — note it and factor it into G4.

**Pass criteria:** Every node flagged at G1 has a contact initiated within the same weekly session. Contacts are logged with date and contact form. Batch contact if necessary — do not leave overdue nodes pending until next week.

**Kill condition:** G2 scan skipped for 2 or more consecutive weeks → the relationship portfolio is in free decay. Resume immediately. Do not compensate with a burst of simultaneous outreach — mass reconnection reads as transactional. Work through the overdue queue sequentially, spaced over several days.

## Gate G3 — Behavior vs. Words Verification

**Trigger:** Any relationship newly classified as "important but unverified" — someone who plays a significant role in your stated plans but whose reliability has not been established through observed behavior over time.

**Action:** Open a 3-month observation window. Track verbal commitments and follow-through systematically.

**Rule (MD-330):** Verbal commitments are hypotheses. Behavior is data. A person's follow-through pattern over 3 months is more predictive of future reliability than any single conversation, warm impression, or stated intention. The goal of this gate is data collection, not impression scoring.

**Tracking protocol:**
1. Record each verbal commitment in specific, observable terms: "I'll introduce you to X by end of month," "I'll review your proposal this week," "Let's schedule a call next Tuesday." Vague expressions of goodwill ("I'll definitely help out") do not count as trackable commitments — they carry no observable verification condition.
2. Observe follow-through: did the committed action happen, happen partially, or not happen? Record the outcome.
3. Track a minimum of 3 separate commitments over the observation window before drawing conclusions. A single data point is insufficient.

**Pass criteria:** At least 3 verbal commitments tracked with fulfillment status logged. Window opens on the date of first commitment observation. Window closes at 3 months or after 5+ commitment data points, whichever comes first.

**Kill condition:** If you are relying on someone's future action in a consequential decision — a deal, a pivot, a referral, a plan that requires their involvement — before the G3 window closes → build a contingency that does not require their follow-through. One verbal commitment is not a guarantee. "He said he'd help, should be fine" is not a plan. It is an unverified hypothesis.

## Gate G4 — Fulfillment Rate Assessment

**Trigger:** After the G3 observation window closes. Also triggered whenever a consequential decision depends on a specific person's reliability, regardless of window status.

**Action:** Calculate the commitment fulfillment rate for each tracked node.

```
commitment_fulfillment_rate = fulfilled_commitments / total_tracked_commitments
```

**Interpretation framework:**

| Fulfillment Rate | Classification | Action |
|-----------------|---------------|--------|
| ≥80% | High-signal, reliable | Weight verbal commitments at face value; invest maintenance attention accordingly |
| 50%–79% | Moderate-signal | Cross-check any critical commitment before depending on it; do not rely on verbal alone for load-bearing plans |
| <50% | Low-signal | Discount verbal commitments significantly; require demonstrated action before building plans around this node |

**Rule (MD-330):** The key metric is fulfillment rate — not impression score, not verbal volume. A charismatic person who commits frequently and delivers rarely is a lower-signal relationship than a quiet person who commits sparingly and delivers consistently. Likability is not a signal multiplier. Treat it as noise until the fulfillment rate confirms it.

**Anti-pattern (MD-330):** "He's nice but never follows through" is exactly the failure mode this gate prevents. Persistent behavioral inconsistency is not a personality trait to forgive — it is a reliable predictor of future behavior. Adjusting the weight is not a judgment; it is calibration.

**Pass criteria:** Every relationship with G3 data has a fulfillment rate calculated and logged. The rate governs how much weight that person's verbal commitments receive in planning decisions. The rate is updated whenever new commitment outcomes are recorded.

**Kill condition:** If a Tier 1 or Tier 2 relationship has a fulfillment rate below 50% after a full observation window → the tier assignment is inconsistent with the behavioral evidence. Either correct the tier downward, or initiate a direct conversation about the gap between stated intentions and observed behavior. Running a Tier 1/2 relationship on low-signal behavioral data is a structural planning error.

## Gate G5 — Relationship Portfolio Review

**Trigger:** Annual. Schedule it explicitly. This review does not occur naturally — it must be calendared.

**Action:** Full audit of the relationship map built at G0. Run three sequential passes.

**Pass 1 — Tier accuracy:**
Review each node. Has their actual role in your life over the past 12 months matched their assigned tier? Promote or demote based on recent behavior, not historical significance or aspirational potential. A Tier 2 node who has provided zero meaningful intellectual, emotional, or practical value in the past year is a Tier 3 node. Reclassify it.

**Pass 2 — Behavioral signal triage:**
For every node with G4 data: does the fulfillment rate support the current tier assignment? Nodes with persistently low fulfillment rates in Tier 1 or 2 are misclassified. Reclassify downward. Nodes with consistently high fulfillment rates sitting in Tier 3 are underclassified. Promote them.

**Pass 3 — Attention reallocation:**
- Identify nodes where you are overinvesting relative to signal return: high maintenance cadence, low relationship value, low behavioral reliability.
- Identify nodes where behavioral signal is high but your maintenance cadence has been insufficient: underinvesting in high-signal relationships.
- Reallocate: reduce cadence on low-signal nodes; increase cadence on high-signal, under-maintained nodes.

**Rule (MD-328):** Relationship attention is finite. Maintaining 20 nodes at Tier 2 cadence is not sustainable without quality degrading across all of them. The annual review is the mechanism for keeping the portfolio sized to what you can actually maintain. A well-maintained portfolio of 12 nodes outperforms a neglected portfolio of 25.

**Pass criteria:** An updated relationship map exists with current tier assignments. At minimum one reallocation decision is made and logged — an upgrade, a downgrade, or a cadence adjustment. "Everything is the same as last year" is not a valid output of an annual review.

**Kill condition:** If the annual review reveals that multiple Tier 1/2 relationships have simultaneously dropped to low-signal status → this is a systemic maintenance failure, not isolated relationship decay. Return to G2 and rebuild the weekly scan habit before attempting G5 again. Portfolio strategy on top of a broken maintenance habit produces no improvement.

## Self-Test Scenario

**Setup:** You have a professional acquaintance — call them Alex — whom you met 18 months ago at a conference. Alex is well-connected in your field. In the first 3 months, Alex said: "I'll introduce you to my former partner who runs the fund you're targeting," "Let's grab coffee next time I'm in town," and "I'll send you that research report I mentioned." None of these materialized. You haven't spoken in 4 months. You are now considering reaching out to Alex because you have a specific ask.

Apply the gates:

**G0 — What tier is Alex?**
Based on the past 18 months of actual interaction — not stated intentions — Alex is a Tier 3 node at best. If Alex was previously listed as Tier 2, the classification is wrong. The tier must reflect actual relationship density, not aspirational density. Correct it before proceeding.

**G1 — Is Alex past the silence ceiling?**
4 months of silence exceeds the Tier 3 ceiling of 3 months. Alex is overdue regardless of tier classification. The G1 flag fires.

**G2 — Should you reach out now?**
Yes — but not with the ask. Proactive maintenance contact and transactional solicitation are separate actions and must not be combined in the same interaction. Initiate non-purposeful contact first: acknowledge something relevant to Alex's recent work, share a resource of interest, or simply re-establish presence with no embedded agenda. If the first contact in 4 months is an ask, you are running the exact transactional model this protocol is designed to prevent.

**G3 — What does the observation window show?**
3 verbal commitments tracked: fund introduction (not fulfilled), coffee meeting (not fulfilled), research report (not fulfilled). Fulfillment rate after 3 data points: 0/3 = 0%.

**G4 — What is Alex's signal classification?**
Fulfillment rate = 0%. Classification: low-signal, discount words significantly. Alex's verbal commitments carry near-zero informational weight. Do not build plans that depend on Alex following through. Any request made should be designed with the assumption that non-delivery is the base case.

**G5 — Portfolio allocation implication:**
Alex belongs in Tier 3 with low maintenance cadence. The behavioral record does not support higher investment. Reallocate the freed attention budget to relationships with demonstrated high fulfillment rates. Continuing to pursue Alex as a high-value connector based on 18-month-old verbal commitments is a resource allocation error.

**Verdict:** Reach out to Alex this week with non-purposeful contact to close the silence gap and maintain the Tier 3 relationship. Do not anchor a consequential goal to Alex's verbal promises. Identify other paths to the fund target that do not route through low-signal intermediaries.

| Condition | Threshold | Response |

| Tier 1 node past silence ceiling | >2 weeks without meaningful contact | Contact immediately. No deferral. |
| 2+ Tier 1 nodes simultaneously overdue | Any time | Re-run G0. Structural neglect — recalibrate maintenance habit before resuming G2. |
| G2 scan skipped | 2+ consecutive weeks | Resume immediately. Do not compensate with burst outreach — work through overdue queue sequentially. |
| Relying on unverified verbal commitment | Before G3 window closes | Build a contingency. One promise is a hypothesis, not a plan. |
