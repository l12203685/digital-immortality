# Twitter Thread — SOP #95: Organism Network Effect Protocol

**Domain:** 4 — 社交圈 / collective intelligence
**Date:** 2026-04-09 UTC (Cycle 260)
**Hook:** "I ran 3 digital models of people I know against the same scenarios. Some divergences appeared in every pair. Those aren't personal differences — they're structural. That's the insight no single relationship can give you."

---

**1/**
I ran digital models of 3 people I know against the same set of scenarios.

Some divergences appeared in every pair.
Some appeared in only one.

The ones that kept appearing?

Those aren't personality differences.

That's a structural fault line in how humans make decisions.

🧵

---

**2/**
One collision (SOP #44) tells you where two people diverge.

That's useful.

But it can't tell you if the divergence is *you and this one person* — or *a category of humans who think this way*.

For that, you need the network.

---

**3/**
The math compounds fast:

2 organisms → 1 pair collision
3 organisms → 3 pairs + 1 triangle
N organisms → N(N-1)/2 pairs

The triangle is the richest unit:

A diverges from B on axis X.
C agrees with A.

Now you know X is a value split — not a quirk.

---

**4/**
The classification rule:

→ Divergence appears in only 1 pair = **IDIOSYNCRATIC** (possibly just those two people)
→ Divergence appears in 2+ pairs, same axis = **STRUCTURAL** (a real fault line worth mapping)

You can't see this with one collision.

You need the pattern.

---

**5/**
What I found running 3 organisms:

`relationship_downgrade` appeared as a divergence in 2 of 3 pairs.

In both cases: I chose explicit renegotiation. They chose quiet withdrawal.

That's not a personality conflict.

That's a genuine policy difference — two complete, coherent systems for handling the same problem.

---

**6/**
Three organisms agreeing on something counterintuitive = high-confidence principle.

Not social desirability.
Not coincidence.
Three independent decision models converging on the same unusual call.

That's worth writing into your own DNA.

---

**7/**
Three organisms diverging on the same axis = something better:

A publishable insight.

Not "here's my opinion on X."

Instead: "On [axis], people systematically differ. Here are the 2-3 policies that exist and the premises behind each."

That's a thread, not a take.

---

**8/**
When to add a 3rd organism:

If your current network has > 90% agreement → add one. You're not generating signal. Too homogeneous.

If < 75% agreement → don't add yet. Calibrate the existing organisms first. Noise, not signal.

The window: 75–90% agree = productive divergence band.

---

**9/**
Organisms decay. Networks decay faster — staleness compounds across pairs.

Rule: any organism uncalibrated > 90 days = stale.

Major life event (job, city, relationship) = likely stale immediately.

Don't run network analysis on stale data. The results are meaningless.

---

**10/**
The output isn't consensus.

It's a divergence map.

Where does the network cluster? Where does it fracture?
Which fault lines are structural vs. idiosyncratic?

That map is more valuable than any single relationship insight.

Build the network. Map the fractures.
