## Introduction

Most people treat relationships as discrete connections. You know Person A. You know Person B. Each relationship is independent. This is wrong — and it's why most people's networks plateau.

Relationships are not discrete — they are a **network**, and networks have emergent properties that discrete connections don't. The most valuable property: **second-order effects**. The friend who introduces you to three of *their* friends does not give you 3 relationships — they give you access to 3 networks, each with their own connectivity. This compounds.

Samuel's DNA is correct: "Relationships are the alpha — network density compounds faster than any investment." Edward's DNA underweights this. This SOP closes that gap formally.

**Core Claim:** A relationship's true value = its direct value + ∑(second-order introductions × their network density). People who understand this invest in relationships differently than people who see only direct value.

DNA anchors: MD-328 (tier classification by contribution, not affection), MD-330 (behavior verification — 3 months before trusting network access), MD-202 (output loop — every tier-1 relationship needs shared output quarterly), MD-55 (compounding begins from first connection, not at threshold).

## Gate G0 — Network Position Audit

**Trigger:** Run quarterly or after any major life event (job change, relocation, project launch).

**Action:** Map your current network across 3 dimensions:

**Dimension 1 — Tier classification (from SOP #19):**
- Tier 1: Core nodes (≤5 people, weekly contact)
- Tier 2: Important nodes (≤20 people, monthly contact)
- Tier 3: Strategic nodes (≤100 people, quarterly contact)

**Dimension 2 — Network density (second-order reach):**
For each Tier 1 and Tier 2 node, estimate:
- How many people does this person know that I don't?
- How many of those are in domains I want to enter?
- Has this person actively introduced me to anyone in the past 12 months?

**Dimension 3 — Introduction velocity:**
- In the past 12 months, how many introductions have you received from your network?
- How many have you made?
- What is your network's introduction:connection ratio?

**Output:** List of nodes with high second-order value (large network × aligned domain × active introducer history) vs nodes with zero second-order value (large network but never introduces anyone).

**Gate pass condition:** You can name ≥3 nodes who have introduced you to others in the past 12 months.

**If no gate pass:** Your network is a static asset, not a compounding one. Proceed to G1 for activation.

## Gate G1 — Compounding Activation

**Trigger:** After G0 audit. Applies to any Tier 1/2 node identified as "high second-order value but inactive introducer."

**Problem:** A node may have massive network density but still not introduce you to anyone. This is the "dormant asset" problem. You have the connection — you're not activating its compounding property.

**Activation sequence:**

**Step 1: Verify behavior (MD-330, 3-month window)**
Before activating a node for introductions, confirm via behavior (not language) that this person:
- Actually introduces people (ask: "who have you introduced to each other recently?")
- Has a reputation as a connector, not a gatekeeper
- Has shared an output with you (article, referral, insight) in the past 6 months

A connector who talks about connecting but never does = 0 second-order value regardless of network size.

**Step 2: Create shared output (MD-202)**
You cannot ask for introductions cold. You need shared output first:
- Co-author something (article, analysis, project)
- Solve a problem together
- Give them credit publicly for an insight they gave you

Shared output creates mutual investment. Mutual investment activates the introduction reflex.

**Step 3: Make the first introduction (reciprocity seeding)**
Before asking for any introduction, make 2-3 introductions for them. Connect them to people in your network they'd benefit from knowing. This:
- Demonstrates you understand introduction value
- Creates reciprocity pressure
- Signals you're a connector, not just a consumer

**Gate pass condition:** Shared output exists + you have made ≥2 introductions for this person.

## Gate G2 — Introduction Request Protocol

**Trigger:** After G1 pass. You are ready to request an introduction.

**The mistake most people make:** "Can you introduce me to [person]?" This is a cold ask that places all the work on the introducer and makes you look needy.

**The correct protocol:**

**Step 1: Specific ask, specific context**
- Wrong: "Can you introduce me to anyone in VC?"
- Right: "I'm exploring early-stage crypto infrastructure. You mentioned [specific person] works in exactly that. Would a 30-minute intro call be appropriate? Here's a 3-sentence context you could forward: [prepared context]"

**Why this works:**
- Specificity signals you've done the work (not fishing)
- Pre-prepared context removes friction from the introducer (they can copy-paste)
- "Would it be appropriate?" respects the introducer's judgment (they know the relationship best)

**Step 2: Make it easy to say yes**
Provide:
- Who you are (1 sentence)
- What you're working on (1 sentence)
- Why this specific connection matters (1 sentence)
- What you're asking for (30-min call, email exchange, etc.)
- Your calendar availability or Calendly link

**Step 3: Close the loop**
After the introduction happens:
- Update the introducer on the outcome (even if nothing came of it)
- Thank them publicly if appropriate (LinkedIn mention, etc.)
- Make a note in your network tracker

**Gate pass condition:** Introduction happened AND you closed the loop with the introducer.

## Gate G3 — Second-Order Activation

**Trigger:** After any successful introduction. This is where compounding begins.

**The second-order activation move:**
When the introduced person becomes a Tier 2/3 connection, immediately audit their network density:
- Do they know people in domains you want to enter?
- Have they introduced others in the past?

If yes: they are now a potential node for future G1 activation. You are now one introduction deeper in the network.

**The compounding math (simple version):**
- Each Tier 1 node who introduces you to 3 people per year = 3 new potential nodes
- If each new node has similar density and also introduces 3 people/year = 9 second-order connections
- At 5 active Tier 1 nodes: 5 × 3 × 3 = 45 second-order introductions per year from your existing 5 Tier 1 relationships

This is why network density compounds. You don't need more Tier 1 relationships — you need to activate the compounding properties of the ones you already have.

**Gate pass condition:** At least 1 new node from a recent introduction has been audited for second-order value and added to your network tracker.

## Gate G4 — Anti-Decay Maintenance

**Trigger:** Quarterly. Prevents relationship network from decaying.

**The decay problem:** Networks don't stay stable — they decay toward your investment rate. If you invest nothing, connections go cold within 6-12 months (varies by platform).

**Maintenance schedule:**
- Tier 1 (≤5): weekly contact minimum, at least 1 shared output/quarter
- Tier 2 (≤20): monthly contact minimum, at least 1 meaningful exchange/quarter
- Tier 3 (≤100): quarterly contact minimum, 1 public signal/quarter (comment, like, share)

**What counts as contact:**
- In-person meeting, call, video call: full credit
- Substantive message (asks about their project, responds to specific content): 80% credit
- Generic "how are you?" or birthday message: 10% credit (does not reset the clock)

**Gate pass condition:** Every Tier 1 and Tier 2 node has received ≥1 substantive contact in the past 30 days.

**Prune signal:** If a Tier 2 node has received no substantive contact from you AND no second-order introduction in 12 months AND you have no planned shared output → downgrade to Tier 3 or remove. Inactive nodes consume attention without returning value.

## Self-Test

**Scenario:** Your Tier 1 contact mentions they recently hired someone who's an expert in a field you want to learn more about. You'd love an introduction but you haven't made any introductions for this person in the past year, and your most recent shared output was 8 months ago.

**Apply this SOP:**
- G0: Node audit — this person has high second-order value (active introducer, dense network)
- G1: Shared output gap (8 months) and reciprocity gap (0 introductions made for them) → reactivate G1 before requesting
- Action: Make 1-2 introductions for them first. Create shared output (send them an article with specific insight; offer to co-write something). Wait for G1 pass condition.
- **Do NOT request the introduction yet.** Cold ask after 8 months of no investment = disrespect for the relationship economy.

**Correct timing:** Make 2 introductions → wait for their response → create shared output → then request the specific introduction with prepared context.
