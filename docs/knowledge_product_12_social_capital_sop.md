# SOP #12 — Social Capital & Network Design

> **Meta-claim**: Relationships are assets. Assets require active management.
> Reactive maintenance is a policy of slow divestment.
> Source MDs: 328, 329, 330 + supporting

---

## Core Claim

Most people manage relationships reactively — they respond when contacted, reconnect when they need something, and let silence accumulate until the relationship quietly expires.

Edward treats relationships the same way he treats a portfolio: each node is an asset with a decay rate, a maintenance cost, and a return profile. Left unattended, the asset depreciates. The management protocol runs on a schedule, not on a feeling.

The same principle applies to entering new knowledge domains and evaluating new relationships: structure before content, behavior before belief.

## The 6 Gates

### G0: Network Asset Inventory
**Rule:** Before managing, enumerate. You cannot maintain what you have not listed. (MD-328)

Action:
1. List 10–15 key relationship nodes by name.
2. Classify each by tier:
   - **Intimate** (close friends, partner, family you actively invest in)
   - **Important friend / peer** (people whose thinking shapes yours; mutual investment)
   - **Career mentor / advisor** (people who can open doors or calibrate your judgment)
3. For each node, record: last contact date, last *you-initiated* contact date, relationship domain (personal / professional / advisory).

**Output:** A living list, not a mental model. If it isn't written down, it doesn't exist as a managed asset.

**Anti-pattern:** "I know who my important relationships are" without a written inventory. Memory systematically biases toward recently active relationships and drops dormant ones — exactly the ones that need maintenance.

### G1: Maintenance Schedule — Silence Thresholds
**Rule:** Set the maximum allowable silence period per tier. Install a weekly scan habit. (MD-328)

**Default silence thresholds:**

| Tier | Max silence period | Contact format |
|------|-------------------|---------------|
| Intimate | 2 weeks | Any — call, message, presence |
| Important friend / peer | 1 month | Message, share, short catch-up |
| Career mentor / advisor | 3 months | Substantive update, question, or share |

**Weekly scan protocol:**
- Every week (pick a fixed day), open the inventory.
- Flag any node whose last contact exceeds its tier threshold.
- Flagged node → trigger a touchpoint before the next weekly scan.

**Touchpoint cost calibration:**
- A forwarded article with one sentence of context costs 45 seconds.
- A "saw this and thought of you" message costs 20 seconds.
- Proactive low-cost contact is not transactional. Its function is maintaining signal presence, not extracting value.

**Test:** Can you look at your inventory right now and confirm no node has exceeded its threshold?
If no → you are already running a relationship deficit.

**Anti-pattern:** Waiting until you *need* something to contact someone. That converts relationship maintenance into a transaction. The person receiving it feels the difference. The relationship decays faster after transactional contact than after no contact at all.

### G2: Framework-First Learning (Domain Entry Protocol)
**Rule:** When entering any new domain for knowledge acquisition — including a domain relevant to a new relationship — build the skeleton before the detail. (MD-329)

This gate exists in an SOP about social capital because the fastest way to add value in a new relationship is to rapidly become informed about the other person's domain. Framework-first is how you do that without wasting time.

**The 20/80 allocation:**
- **20% of time** → build the skeleton (TOC, survey articles, overview chapters). Do not skip this phase even when it feels unproductive.
- **80% of time** → fill in details with anchor points already established.

**Framework-building sequence:**
1. Find the map before reading the territory: index / table of contents / Wikipedia overview / domain survey article.
2. Identify 3–5 core concepts and the relationships between them.
3. Sketch a rough concept map (handwritten is fine; precision is not the goal at this stage).
4. Only then enter deep reading — each detail now has a slot to attach to.

**Test:** Before going deep, can you name the 3–5 central concepts of this domain and explain how they relate to each other in 2–3 sentences?
If no → you do not have a skeleton yet. Do not proceed to detail.

**Anti-pattern:** "I'll read the first chapter and see where it goes." Linear entry without a prior map means every piece of information is stored in isolation. Retrieval rate approaches zero. You will have read the book and retained the feeling of having learned, not the knowledge.

### G3: Signal Calibration — Behavior Over Speech
**Rule:** For any new relationship: establish a 3-month observation window. Track commitment fulfillment rate. Weight behavior; discount speech. (MD-330)

**Why this gate exists:**
Language is cheap. Enthusiasm in conversation costs nothing. Behavior — showing up, following through, doing the thing they said they would do — is paid for with real resources (time, attention, social capital). When the two signals diverge, the behavioral signal carries almost all the information.

**Operating protocol:**
1. When entering any new relationship with material consequence (potential partner, collaborator, mentor, hire), activate the 3-month observation window from first substantive contact.
2. Log verbal commitments as they occur. Do not rely on memory. A notes entry per commitment is sufficient: date, what was promised, deadline if stated.
3. Track follow-through: did the action happen? On time? Without reminders?
4. Calculate commitment fulfillment rate at the 3-month mark:

```
Fulfillment rate = (commitments followed through) / (commitments made) × 100%
```

**Decision thresholds:**

| Rate | Interpretation | Action |
|------|---------------|--------|
| ≥ 80% | Speech and behavior aligned; high signal reliability | Weight verbal commitments at face value |
| 50–79% | Partial alignment; person is willing but inconsistent | Continue observing; do not weight future verbal commitments heavily |
| < 50% over 3 months | Structural gap between speech and behavior | Discount speech weight to near-zero; make no plans that depend on their follow-through |

**Clarification:** A fulfillment rate < 50% is not a moral judgment. It is a calibration. Some people are optimistic commiters with poor execution. Others are episodically reliable. The rate tells you what kind of reliance is appropriate — not whether the person is worth knowing.

**Anti-pattern:** "He said he would help, so I assumed it was handled." Single verbal commitments are not evidence. They are hypotheses. The follow-through is the data point.

### G4: Proactive Contact Protocol — Monthly Trigger
**Rule:** Monthly scan output determines action. Silence past threshold = trigger. Content is not required to be "useful." (MD-328)

**Monthly protocol:**
1. Review the G0 inventory.
2. For each node past its silence threshold, draft a touchpoint.
3. Touchpoint criteria: it must be sent. It does not need to:
   - Be informative
   - Be long
   - Reference a need or ask
   - Have a "reason" beyond maintaining the connection

**Acceptable touchpoint formats:**
- "Saw this [link] and thought of your work on X."
- "How's [project / city / thing they mentioned last time]?"
- "Was thinking about [conversation we had] — wanted to share [brief update]."
- "It's been too long. Hope you're well."

**What this is not:** Networking. Networking implies instrumental contact — I contact you because you may be useful. This protocol is maintenance of existing valued relationships regardless of immediate utility. The return on maintenance is distributed across time, not collected at the point of contact.

**Frequency of explicit asks:** No rule on minimum, but a strong rule on sequencing. Do not make an ask in the first touchpoint after an extended silence. Re-establish presence first.

**Test:** Is there a node in your G0 list that, if they asked "how have you been?", you would feel slight guilt about not having been in touch? That node is past threshold.

### G5: Annual Network Audit — Composition vs. Current Goals
**Rule:** Annually, re-evaluate whether your network composition matches your current-year goals. Prune nodes that no longer fit. Add nodes that are missing. (MD-328, MD-330)

**Why annual, not ongoing:**
Network composition should be stable within a year. Constant re-evaluation creates churn and prevents relationship depth. But goals shift. The network that served your 2023 goals may be misaligned for 2026.

**Audit questions:**
1. **Coverage**: For each major goal this year, do you have at least 2–3 nodes with direct relevant experience or access?
2. **Density vs. breadth**: Are you over-concentrated in one domain (e.g., all finance, no tech)? Is that intentional?
3. **Tier decay**: Are any nodes classified as "intimate" or "important" where the fulfillment rate (G3) has been consistently low? Re-tier appropriately.
4. **Missing nodes**: What type of person, if added, would most accelerate your current primary goal? Do you have a path to meeting one?
5. **Prune candidates**: Are there nodes on your list that you are maintaining out of inertia rather than genuine relationship value? Pruning is not cutting off — it is reducing maintenance investment to match actual relationship tier.

**Output:** An updated G0 inventory with revised tiers, adjusted silence thresholds, and a list of 1–3 relationship types to actively cultivate in the next year.

**Anti-pattern:** "I'll network more." That is a volume strategy applied to a composition problem. The question is not how many people you contact — it is whether the nodes you are maintaining align with where you are going.

## Self-Test Scenario

**Situation:** Edward meets a potential trading partner — call him Alex — at a conference. Alex is enthusiastic, speaks fluently about strategy alignment, proposes a formal collaboration by the end of the first conversation, and offers to introduce Edward to three other traders he knows.

Walk through the gates:

**G0 — Inventory:** Alex is not yet on the inventory. Do not add him as a high-tier node based on one conversation. Flag as "pending observation" — a holding status before placement.

**G1 — Schedule:** No maintenance schedule until placement is determined. The observation window (G3) must close first.

**G2 — Framework-first learning:** Alex operates in a domain adjacent to Edward's. Before the next substantive conversation, Edward spends 20% of his available time building a skeleton of Alex's stated edge (e.g., if Alex focuses on options market-making, Edward maps the 3–5 core concepts of that approach). This allows real conversation rather than surface-level exchange — and signals genuine interest, which is itself a relationship investment.

**G3 — Signal calibration:** Activate the 3-month observation window from the day they met.
- Log verbal commitments made at the conference:
  - "I'll send you my framework doc this week" → did it arrive?
  - "I'll introduce you to [name]" → did the introduction happen?
  - "Let's set up a call to go deeper" → did Alex initiate, or did Edward have to chase?
- At 3 months, calculate fulfillment rate.
- If fulfillment rate ≥ 80%: Alex's speech carries weight. The proposed collaboration has a behavioral foundation.
- If fulfillment rate < 50%: The enthusiasm was real, the execution is not. Do not enter a collaboration that depends on Alex following through. Discount the proposal accordingly.

**G4 — Contact protocol:** During the observation window, maintain presence without pressure. One touchpoint per month — a relevant article share, a brief update on something discussed at the conference. This is maintenance during the observation period, not a demand for commitment fulfillment.

**G5 — Audit (annual):** At year-end audit, if Alex's fulfillment rate qualified him for placement in the career advisory tier, assess whether that tier has appropriate coverage. If the relationship has deepened and he belongs in the peer tier, re-tier. If the collaboration never materialized and contact has been one-directional, reduce to lower tier or prune.

**Outcome logic:** The enthusiasm at the conference was data about Alex's communication style, not about his reliability. The 3-month observation produces the reliability data. The collaboration decision is made on the behavioral record, not the first impression.

## Common Failure Modes

| Failure | Diagnosis | Fix |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Important relationship has gone 6 months silent | G1 not installed — no weekly scan | Set a recurring weekly calendar event; make inventory scan the first action |
| Relied on someone who didn't follow through | Skipped G3 — trusted speech over behavior | Install 3-month observation window for all consequential new relationships |
| Learned a lot about a new domain but can't retrieve it | Skipped G2 — consumed detail without skeleton | For any new domain, spend first 20% of time on the map, not the content |
| Network is large but nobody can help with current goals | Skipped G5 — maintaining inertia network | Run annual audit against current-year goals; identify composition gaps |
| Reach out only when you need something | G4 not running — contact is transactional | Install monthly scan; separate touchpoints from requests |
| Collaboration agreement fell apart on execution | Skipped G3 thresholds — acted on enthusiasm | Check fulfillment rate before any structural commitment |
